My Definition of Mixed Methods Research

My understanding of Mixed Methods Research (MMR) emerged from the tension between my hard sciences training and my K-12 teaching experiences. Quantitative methods taught me to identify patterns and describe what was happening with precision. However, in classrooms, I discovered that test scores and intervention outcomes could not capture why students struggled or succeeded in ways that contradicted those patterns. While I now lean qualitative, I have not abandoned my quantitative training. Instead, I seek to integrate both perspectives to generate richer understanding.

My definition of mixed methods research is:

Mixed methods research is the systematic integration of qualitative and quantitative methods at the level of research questions, data collection, and analysis, wherein quantitative approaches describe what is occurring and qualitative approaches explain why. Like a complex mathematical function that requires both its algebraic form and plotted values to reveal its full behavior across contexts, MMR methods generate comprehensive understanding that neither could achieve independently.

This understanding rests on a philosophical foundation that values multiple ways of knowing. Greene (2007) conceptualizes MMR as inviting “multiple mental models into the same inquiry space for purposes of respectful conversation, dialogue, and learning one from the other” (p. 13). Rather than treating qualitative and quantitative methods as merely different data types, her framework recognizes them as fundamentally different ways of thinking about phenomena. This concept directly informs my requirement that methods work together like a mathematical function’s components—neither privileged, but in dialogue to generate comprehensive understanding. Her emphasis on “purposeful mixing” matters precisely because it allows context-appropriate designs while maintaining intentionality, without prescribing which specific stages require integration.

Moving from philosophical grounding to practical implementation, I find that MMR must balance rigor with feasibility. Tashakkori and Creswell (2007) articulate this through concrete actions: investigators “collect and analyze data, integrate the findings, and draw inferences using both qualitative and quantitative approaches” (p. 4). Having taught in resource-limited schools, I appreciate their acknowledgment that MMR must be implementable within real constraints. Yet where they prioritize inclusiveness, my definition establishes tighter boundaries. In my view, simply collecting both data types falls short of genuine MMR; integration must occur at the methods level—shaping research questions, informing data collection, and structuring analysis—not merely appearing when researchers draw final conclusions.

The power of this methodological integration lies in what it produces. Johnson, Onwuegbuzie, and Turner (2007) emphasize that MMR “combines elements of qualitative and quantitative research approaches... for the broad purposes of breadth and depth of understanding and corroboration” (p. 123). This combination resonates with my mathematical training: quantitative methods describe variables and reveal what is happening across cases (breadth), while qualitative methods elucidate functional relationships and explain why phenomena operate as they do (depth). The analogy is precise—without understanding a function’s algebraic form, you cannot predict its behavior across domains; without plotting actual values, you cannot visualize or verify those predictions. What Johnson et al. call “corroboration” captures this essential dynamic: methods don’t simply coexist; they actively test and refine each other.

These requirements for integration create clear boundaries around what constitutes MMR. When Morse and Niehaus (2009) define mixed methods as “a systematic way of using two or more research methods to answer a single research question” (p. 9), they emphasize integration of methods rather than merely data—a crucial distinction. Not every study collecting both qualitative and quantitative data constitutes MMR in my view. Integration must occur systematically at the methods level throughout the research process, not just at final interpretation. Studies where qualitative and quantitative strands never “converse”—where they remain in separate analytical silos until a discussion section attempts to reconcile them—represent multiple methods, not mixed methods.

Yet boundaries without justification become arbitrary. Morgan (2014) provides the pragmatic rationale for why integration matters: MMR designs leverage “the combined strengths of qualitative and quantitative methods” to “accomplish more than would have been possible with one method alone” (p. xiii). This value-added perspective cuts through abstract debates about methodology. In K-12 classrooms, I couldn’t afford philosophical purity—I needed tools to understand why students struggled despite positive test scores, or why interventions supported by quantitative evidence failed in practice. Morgan’s framing validates my definition’s insistence on genuine integration because integration is precisely where additional insight emerges, where the synthesis produces understanding unavailable to either approach alone. Effective MMR balances Tashakkori and Creswell’s (2007) concern for feasibility with Morse and Niehaus’s (2009) requirement for systematic integration.

Drawing these threads together, my definition insists on several non-negotiable elements: systematic integration at the methods level (Morse & Niehaus, 2009), purposeful mixing from design through analysis (Greene, 2007), attention to both breadth and depth (Johnson et al., 2007), and pragmatic feasibility (Tashakkori & Creswell, 2007; Morgan, 2014). Equally important is what it excludes—studies that merely collect both data types without methods-level integration, sequential designs where strands never meaningfully engage each other, and multi-method approaches that lack systematic integration across phases. These boundaries emerge from my dual experience in hard sciences and K-12 teaching, where I learned that genuine insight comes from integration between methods, not from accumulating multiple data sources. Like a mathematical function, MMR requires both components in active relationship—informing, testing, and refining each other across contexts—to generate the understanding that justifies its complexity.
