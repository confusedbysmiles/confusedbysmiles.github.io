Concurrent Convergent Mixed Methods Design for Technology-Mediated Student Agency in Mathematics: The Student Agency Math Problem Generator
Strand 1: Quantitative
(Correlational Design)
RQs
What patterns emerge in student usage, problem selection, and completion behaviors when using the Student Agency Math Problem Generator?
Sample
Convenience sample of 15-25 secondary math students (grades 6-12) underserved by traditional instruction, recruited from researcher's school district in Omaha metro area
Data Collection
Weeks 1-8 (Concurrent Collection)
  • - App-embedded analytics (browser storage): topic selection, difficulty level, context choices, completion rates, time on task, return visits
  • - Automated tracking throughout intervention period
  • - Weekly data exports for ongoing analysis
Data Analysis
  • - Descriptive statistics, frequency distributions
  • - Correlation analysis (Pearson's r)
  • - Pattern analysis across time
Strand 2: Qualitative
(Phenomenological Approach)
Central RQ
How do students underserved by traditional math instruction experience agency, relevance, and mathematical identity when using the Student Agency Math Problem Generator?
Sample
Maximum variation purposive sample of 9-12 students drawn from quantitative participants. Selection based on diverse usage patterns (high/low engagement) and demographics
Data Collection
Weeks 3-7 (Concurrent Collection)
  • - Semi-structured interviews via Zoom (30-45 min each)
  • - Protocol topics: choice experiences, perceived relevance, sense of agency, barriers/affordances, mathematical identity shifts
  • - Screen-capture observations during app use
Data Analysis
  • - Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke)
  • - Inductive coding, constant comparison
  • - Member checking for validation
Mixed Methods (Convergent Design)
RQ
How does the Student Agency Math Problem Generator influence student engagement and perceptions of agency in mathematics learning among students underserved by traditional math instruction?
Integration
Joint display comparing quantitative usage patterns (WHAT students do: selections, completion rates, engagement metrics) with qualitative themes about agency and relevance (WHY they make those choices). Analysis of convergence, divergence, and complementarity generates comprehensive understanding of how technology-mediated student agency operates in mathematics learning.
Note. Qualitative participants are drawn from the quantitative sample (nested design). Data collection occurs concurrently during Weeks 1-8 of the intervention. Integration emphasizes the complementarity of methods: quantitative data reveals what students do; qualitative data explains why they make those choices.
